\begin{abstract}
This doctoral project aims at proposing new statistical learning based methods to solve linear inverse problems, and more specifically image reconstruction. The focus will be laid on the possibility to perform the training stage without ground truth images, provided that suited sets of noisy data are available, as pioneered by the authors of Noise2noise. A second step of the project will be to consider Positron Emission Tomography (PET) imaging as a specific application case, in collaboration with Nantes CHU. In this context, list mode data can be split into statistically independent subsets in many ways, which could yield a tailored version of Noise2noise with enhanced properties.
\end{abstract}

\section{Context}

Producing large databases of ground truth images is however challenging in many
scientific areas, especially medical imaging.
In the medical field, producing high-quality images can be time-consuming and can put the patient at risk (e.g. injecting higher radiotracer doses into the patient as for nuclear imaging).
Getting high-quality images is therefore impossible and learning methods must be carried out without ground truth.
Fortunately, Image restoration, ranging from image denoising to image reconstruction \
based on deep learning without ground truth is an intense field of research;
there are a lot of unsupervised methods to tackle this data limitation that we are going to review.

\subsection{Linear inverse problems}

We are going to study a specific class of problems which is inverse problems. These problems aim to recover a signal $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$, transformed by a direct forward operation
$\mathcal{A}$, given a measurement $y \in \mathbb{R}^d$:

\begin{equation}
    y = \mathcal{A}x
\end{equation}

An inverse problem is assumed to be well-posed if it satisfies the following three properties.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Existence. $\forall y, \exists x$ such as $y = \mathcal{A}x$.
    \item Uniquenness. $\forall y, \exists! x$, such as $y = \mathcal{A}x$.
    \item Stability. $\mathcal{A}^{-1}$ is continuous.
\end{enumerate}

In our situation, the inverse problem is ill-posed, meaning that the one of the above properties is not satisfied.
To tackle this issue, one may consider regularizing the problem by adding prior knowledge on the solution.
In the context of image reconstruction, the forward operator $\mathcal{A}$ is often linear, and the measurements are corrupted by noise.
The linear inverse problem can then be written as:

\begin{equation}
    y = Ax + \epsilon
\end{equation}

with $A \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times n}$ the linear forward operator and $\epsilon \in \mathbb{R}^d$ the noise with prior distribution $p_{\epsilon}$.
